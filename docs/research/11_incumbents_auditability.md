# Incumbent Consultants & Auditability Requirements Research
## For My Green Squares (MGS) — LCA & Mass Energy Balance Platform Strategy

*Research date: March 2026*

---

## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This report covers two strategic intelligence areas for MGS:

1. **Incumbent landscape**: Which sustainability consultants and platforms major UK retailers and FMCG brands currently use for LCA, carbon reporting, and Scope 3 calculations
2. **Auditability requirements**: What Big 4 auditors and assurance standards require from carbon and energy data under CSRD, UK SDR, ISAE 3410/ISSA 5000

**Key findings for "land and expand" strategy:**
- The incumbent ecosystem is fragmented: **Anthesis** dominates UK retail supply chain consultancy (confirmed Tesco partner since 2014); **ERM CVS** dominates standalone GHG verification (confirmed Sainsbury's, Tesco); **Deloitte** and **PwC** dominate integrated audit+assurance (M&S/Deloitte, Diageo/PwC); **Mondra** has become the dominant product-level carbon footprinting platform for UK food retail (M&S, Waitrose, plus 85%+ of UK grocery sector)
- **Manufacture 2030** is the dominant supply-chain data collection platform for Scope 3, embedded in Morrisons, Unilever, and M&S — and is partnered with Mondra
- **South Pole** has lost credibility due to the Kariba REDD+ scandal (2023) — creating an opening for alternatives in strategy/offsetting advisory
- Auditors under **ISAE 3410** (being replaced by **ISSA 5000** from Dec 2026) require full traceability from raw activity data through emission factors to final totals, versioned methodologies, and explicit data quality scoring; Scope 3/product LCA is the weakest point in every company's assurance — this is MGS's entry point
- **CSRD** mandates limited assurance from 2025 for Wave 1 companies; the transition to reasonable assurance is now likely deferred following the Omnibus simplification (2025-2026) — but the structural demand for audit-grade calculation systems remains

## PART 4: BIG 4 AUDITABILITY REQUIREMENTS

### 4.1 CSRD Assurance Requirements (EU)

**Status as of March 2026:**

The EU's [Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)](https://www.geofluxus.com/blog/csrd-timeline-eu-delays) entered effect in phases:

| Wave | Companies | First Report | Omnibus Status |
|------|-----------|--------------|----------------|
| Wave 1 (former NFRD) | Large public-interest entities >500 employees | 2025 (FY2024 data) | Unchanged — already reporting |
| Wave 2 (other large EU) | >1,000 employees; >€450M revenue (post-Omnibus) | 2028 (FY2027) | Delayed 2 years by Stop-the-Clock Directive (April 2025) |
| Wave 3 (listed SMEs) | Now largely excluded post-Omnibus | 2028+ (if applicable) | Scope reduced |
| Wave 4 (non-EU) | >€150M EU revenue; EU subsidiary | 2029 (FY2028) | Largely unchanged |

**Assurance level:**

Per [Deloitte CSRD comparison](https://dart.deloitte.com/USDART/home/publications/deloitte/sustainability-spotlight/2025/sustainability-related-reporting-requirements-and-standards) and [analysis of 1,100 CSRD reports (Trellis, March 2026)](https://trellis.net/article/lessons-1100-csrd-reports/):

- **Currently required**: **Limited assurance** from the first year of CSRD reporting
- Limited assurance = a lighter-touch review that checks for "signs of problems" without confirming numbers are accurate (negative assurance statement: "nothing came to our attention...")
- **Future trajectory**: The original CSRD intended to transition to **reasonable assurance**; however, the **Omnibus II package (2025-2026) eliminates the mandatory transition to reasonable assurance** and removes the obligation to introduce European Sustainability Assurance Standards by 2026. The EC will instead publish targeted guidelines by 2026 clarifying limited assurance procedures.
- In practice, the [Trellis March 2026 analysis](https://trellis.net/article/lessons-1100-csrd-reports/) of 1,100 Wave 1 CSRD reports found companies are **"overwhelmingly using Big Four accounting firms — KPMG, PwC, EY and Deloitte"** for assurance; a small number voluntarily chose reasonable or hybrid assurance

**What CSRD assurance covers:**
1. The sustainability statement's compliance with ESRS
2. The double materiality assessment process
3. Compliance with electronic tagging requirements
4. Compliance with EU Taxonomy Article 8 reporting

**Key impact from early CSRD filings (FY2024):**
- Scope 3 disclosures carry more inherent limitations than Scopes 1-2 — auditors are already flagging this
- Many companies restated sustainability numbers when moving to standardised/assured data
- Forward-looking statements (transition plans, targets, scenarios) flagged as inherently uncertain
- Average sustainability statement length: 70-80 pages; many companies above 95% chose climate (E1) and social (S1) topic standards

### 4.2 UK Sustainability Disclosure Requirements (SDR) — Status 2026

Per [UK Government guidance](https://www.gov.uk/guidance/uk-sustainability-reporting-standards) (updated 25 February 2026) and [Generation Impact analysis](https://generationimpact.global/news/uk-esg-law-2026-uk-srs-fca-cp26-5/):

**What happened:**
- 25 February 2026: UK government (DBT) published **finalised UK Sustainability Reporting Standards (UK SRS S1 and S2)** — the UK-endorsed versions of IFRS S1 and S2, with only six minor UK-specific amendments
- Currently: Available for **voluntary use** by any entity
- FCA Consultation Paper **CP26/5** (January 2026) proposes making UK SRS S2 (climate) **mandatory for listed companies from 1 January 2027** under revised UK Listing Rules

**UK SRS vs CSRD:**

| Dimension | UK SRS (ISSB-based) | CSRD (ESRS-based) |
|-----------|---------------------|-------------------|
| Materiality | Financial/single materiality (investor-focused) | Double materiality (inside-out + outside-in) |
| Scope | Climate first; broader sustainability follows | Full ESG from start |
| Scope 3 | Comply-or-explain initially; FCA did NOT propose mandatory Scope 3 in CP26/5 | Mandatory Scope 3 reporting |
| Assurance | Regime being established (ISSA UK 5000 planned) | Limited assurance mandatory from first year |
| Initial target | Listed companies (UK Listing Rules) | EU-based and EU-revenue entities |

**Assurance for UK SRS:**
- The UK government consulted on the development of an oversight regime for assurance of sustainability-related financial disclosures (consultation closed September 2025)
- The planned standard is **ISSA UK 5000** (UK adoption of ISSA 5000) — regime being established
- No mandatory assurance requirement yet for UK SRS (as of March 2026); this will be set by FCA rules post-CP26/5

**For UK retailers specifically**: Most are already producing sustainability reports with third-party assurance under ISAE 3000/3410 (voluntary) or for CDP purposes. Listed retailers (Tesco, Sainsbury's, M&S) will be early adopters of UK SRS S2 via FCA Listing Rules from 2027.

### 4.3 ISAE 3410 — Assurance on GHG Statements (and Transition to ISSA 5000)

**Current status:** [ISAE 3410](https://ciferi.com/blog/isae-3410-greenhouse-gas-statements-guide) remains in force until its withdrawal date aligned with ISSA 5000's effective date of **15 December 2026**. ISAE 3410 was approved by the IAASB in March 2012.

**What ISAE 3410 requires:**

*Scope determination:*
- Scope 1, 2, and 3 emissions have fundamentally different work effort requirements
- Scope 1: Moderate complexity — direct measurement or calculation from fuel data; requires engineering expertise for complex processes; site visits typically essential for reasonable assurance
- Scope 2: Lower complexity — calculation from energy consumption × grid factors; market-based requires verification of contractual instruments
- Scope 3 / Product LCA: High complexity — relies on estimates, third-party data, industry averages, modelling; practitioner must evaluate reasonableness of assumptions rather than verify source data directly

*Two key uncertainty concepts:*
- **Scientific uncertainty**: Inherent in GHG science (emission factors have inherent uncertainty ranges) — cannot be reduced by better data; must be disclosed in assurance report
- **Estimation uncertainty**: From entity's measurement, data collection, and calculation processes — this is what the auditor focuses on reducing

*Materiality:*
- No prescribed threshold; commonly 5-10% for reasonable assurance
- Different components may warrant different materiality (e.g., 5% error in regulated Scope 1 more material than same % in Scope 3 business travel estimates)

*For product-level LCA specifically:*
- ISAE 3410 does not have explicit product-level PCF rules but the principles apply
- Emission factors must be evaluated for appropriateness; verifiable source (IPCC, national inventories, EU MRR, ecoinvent)
- Calculations must be recalculable from activity data × emission factors × GWP with verified unit conversions
- Completeness must be assessed — all life cycle stages included?

**ISSA 5000 — The New Standard (effective 15 December 2026):**

Per [IAASB ISSA 5000](https://www.iaasb.org/focus-areas/understanding-international-standard-sustainability-assurance-5000):
- Approved September 2024; published November 2024; effective 15 December 2026 (early adoption permitted)
- Replaces ISAE 3000 (Revised) as the primary framework; **ISAE 3410 is withdrawn** on the same date
- More than double the requirements of ISAE 3000
- Key additions vs ISAE 3410:
  - **Double materiality** — requires assessment of both financial materiality and impact materiality (relevant for CSRD)
  - **Value chain considerations** — enhanced guidance on Scope 3 and value chain emissions; directly relevant to product LCA
  - **Qualitative disclosures** — covers narrative sustainability disclosures accompanying GHG data
  - **Broader scope** — all sustainability information, not just GHG
  - **Standalone** — does not require separate compliance with ISAE 3000
- Both limited and reasonable assurance engagements remain; definitions unchanged

### 4.4 Practical Data Requirements for Auditors

Based on synthesis of KPMG, Deloitte, PwC, EY guidance and ISAE 3410/ISSA 5000 requirements:

**Data format and granularity requirements:**

| Data Type | What Auditors Need | Why |
|-----------|-------------------|-----|
| Activity data | Quantity data (kWh, litres, tonnes, km) tied to specific source records (invoices, meter readings, production logs) | Auditors agree to source records and test mathematical accuracy |
| Emission factors | Source identified (IPCC, national inventory, ecoinvent, EU MRR default, entity-specific); version and date; justification for choice | Auditors evaluate appropriateness; must verify source; check if entity-specific factors available and should be used |
| Calculations | Documented formula: Activity data × EF × GWP; unit conversions explicit; aggregation traceable | Auditors recalculate to verify |
| Scope 2 market-based | Evidence of contractual instruments (PPAs, GOOs, RECs) with hourly matching (proposed GHG Protocol Scope 2 revision) | Tighter criteria coming; certificates must be deliverable to buyer's grid region |
| Scope 3 estimates | Methodology and assumptions documented; data sources cited; sensitivity analysis for key assumptions | Auditors assess reasonableness; cannot verify third-party data directly |
| Comparative data | Prior period data available; restatements documented with reasons | ISAE 3410 requires evaluation of consistency |

**The audit trail requirement (KPMG, 2026):**

> "Treat GHG data like financial data: assign clear ownership, use controlled and versioned methodologies, ensure full traceability from raw activity data through emission factors to final totals, and maintain an auditable record of all changes."

This means:
1. **Version control** on all calculation methodologies
2. **Change log** — every data change recorded with timestamp and user
3. **Data lineage** — ability to trace any reported figure back to raw input
4. **Emission factor provenance** — know which version of which database was used for every EF
5. **Boundary documentation** — explicit organisational and operational boundary definition

**Primary vs secondary data treatment:**

| Data Type | Auditor Treatment | Hierarchy |
|-----------|------------------|-----------|
| Primary (measured, metered) | Highest quality; auditor can verify against source documents | Preferred for Scope 1; critical for Scope 2 |
| Primary (supplier-reported) | Good quality if supplier has own controls; auditor evaluates supplier's process | Critical path for Scope 3 Cat 1 improvement |
| Secondary (industry average EFs) | Acceptable for Scope 3 categories where primary unavailable; auditor evaluates appropriateness | Baseline for Scope 3; must be representative |
| Spend-based (economic I-O) | Lowest quality; highest uncertainty; used only where no better data available | Last resort for Scope 3 |

**Common audit findings and failures (based on KPMG 2026, Deloitte surveys):**
1. **Scope 3 data gaps** — categories excluded without justification; especially purchased goods & services (Cat 1) where most food/retail emissions lie
2. **Emission factor inconsistency** — different versions used across years without documentation; factors applied to wrong activity categories
3. **Data quality labelling absent** — no documentation of what is primary vs secondary data; no uncertainty ranges
4. **Controls absent** — no internal sign-off process on GHG data; sustainability team separate from finance controls
5. **Restatements undocumented** — prior year data changed without transparent explanation
6. **Scope 2 market-based errors** — renewable energy certificates not matching consumption period; PPAs not meeting additionality criteria
7. **Forward-looking claims unsupported** — transition plans and net zero targets not supported by documented decarbonisation pathways
8. **Deloitte CEO survey (2024)**: 57% of executives cite data quality as top challenge; 88% cite it as a top-three challenge; 81% report documentation and sign-off as a top challenge

**What makes a carbon calculation "assurable":**

A calculation is assurable when:
- It follows a documented, consistently applied methodology aligned with a recognised standard (GHG Protocol, ISO 14064-1, ESRS E1)
- Activity data can be traced to source records
- Emission factors are sourced from recognised databases with version control
- The calculation is reproducible (auditor can recalculate)
- Boundaries are clearly defined and applied consistently
- Uncertainties are disclosed and quantified
- Changes from prior periods are explained

## PART 5: IMPLICATIONS FOR MGS

### 5.1 What the MGS LCA System Must Produce to Be Auditor-Ready

Based on the requirements analysis above, MGS should design its platform to produce the following for every calculation:

**Calculation outputs (minimum for limited assurance):**

| Output | Specification |
|--------|---------------|
| **Activity data** | Quantity + unit + source identifier (e.g., site ID, meter ID, invoice reference) + period |
| **Emission factor** | EF value + unit + database source (ecoinvent version, DEFRA, IPCC, EU MRR) + date applied + version |
| **Calculation trace** | Activity data × EF × GWP = emissions (explicit, recalculable) |
| **Data quality score** | Primary/secondary/estimated flag per data point; recommended to use data quality scoring system (e.g., ISO 14064-1 DQI or ecoinvent pedigree matrix) |
| **Uncertainty range** | Quantified uncertainty (%) on final output, especially for estimated data |
| **Boundary definition** | System boundary applied (gate-to-gate, cradle-to-gate, cradle-to-grave); allocation method |
| **Methodology reference** | Which standard/methodology applied (ISO 14040/44, GHG Protocol, PACT, PEF) |
| **Timestamp and user** | Who ran the calculation, when, on what data vintage |

**Audit trail requirements:**

1. **Immutable log** of all calculations with timestamps
2. **Version control** — if an emission factor or input changes, prior calculations remain retrievable; new version creates new record
3. **Change documentation** — any restatement or recalculation records the reason, approved by named user
4. **Export capability** — auditor must be able to extract calculation details in machine-readable format
5. **Comparison function** — year-on-year comparison with explanation of differences

### 5.2 Documentation and Metadata Accompanying Each Calculation

Every calculation output from MGS should include:

```
CALCULATION METADATA RECORD

Product/Process ID: [unique identifier]
Reporting period: [start date] — [end date]
Standard applied: [ISO 14040, GHG Protocol Scope 3 Cat 1, PACT, PEF]
Functional unit: [e.g., 1 kg of product at factory gate]
System boundary: [cradle-to-gate / gate-to-gate / cradle-to-grave]
Allocation method: [mass / economic / none applied]

INPUTS:
  Activity data:
    - Item: [e.g., electricity consumption]
    - Value: 1,234 kWh
    - Source type: PRIMARY (metered) | SECONDARY (supplier-reported) | ESTIMATED
    - Source reference: [meter ID / invoice number / database used]
    - Period: [date range]

  Emission factors applied:
    - EF for electricity: 0.233 kgCO2e/kWh
    - Source: DEFRA UK grid average FY2024/25 (version 1.1, published June 2024)
    - [URL or reference]

OUTPUTS:
  GHG result: 287.2 kgCO2e
  Scope boundary: [Scope 1 / Scope 2 / Scope 3 Cat 1]
  Data quality score: 3/5 (secondary supplier data; limited verification)
  Uncertainty (±): 15% [based on supplier data quality]

AUDIT FIELDS:
  Calculated by: [user ID]
  Calculated on: [datetime]
  Approved by: [user ID]
  Approved on: [datetime]
  Methodology version: v2.3
  Previous period value: 301.4 kgCO2e (FY2023/24)
  Change explanation: [if >5% change]
```

### 5.3 Handling Uncertainty and Data Quality Scores

**Recommended framework (aligned with ISAE 3410 and ISO 14044):**

| Level | Definition | Typical sources | Auditor treatment |
|-------|------------|-----------------|-------------------|
| DQ1 — Primary measured | Direct metering, direct measurement | Own meters, own fuel records | Highest confidence; verify against source documents |
| DQ2 — Primary calculated | Calculated from verified primary inputs | Mass balance from verified production data | High confidence; verify calculation logic |
| DQ3 — Supplier-reported | Data reported by supplier, unverified | Supplier carbon footprint data | Moderate confidence; auditor may ask about supplier's own controls |
| DQ4 — Industry average | Published database average (ecoinvent, DEFRA, IPCC) | Generic emission factors | Acceptable for Scope 3 estimation; auditor evaluates representativeness |
| DQ5 — Spend-based proxy | Economic input-output model | Spend data × sector EF | Lowest quality; flagged in assurance; acceptable only where better data unavailable |

**MGS system design implications:**
- Tag every data input with a DQ level
- Calculate a weighted average DQ score for each product footprint
- Surface the percentage of the footprint covered by DQ1-DQ2 (primary) vs DQ3-DQ5 (estimated)
- As mass energy balance platforms generate real process data, they intrinsically improve DQ level from 4→1-2, which is the core value proposition for audit-readiness

**On scientific uncertainty:**
- ISAE 3410 (and ISSA 5000) require the assurance report to include a reference to inherent uncertainty — this is mandatory regardless of data quality
- MGS should provide uncertainty bands on outputs (Monte Carlo or conservative range), not just point estimates
- This supports the auditor's required disclosure and makes the platform more credible

### 5.4 Strategic Positioning: "Land and Expand" Through Auditability

**The audit-readiness gap is MGS's primary entry point:**

The Deloitte 2024 survey found 57% of executives cite data quality as the top ESG challenge and 81% report documentation/sign-off as a top challenge. The [KPMG 2026 GHG accounting outlook](https://kpmg.com/ee/en/insights/2026/01/Greenhouse-gas-accounting-in-2025-and-beyond--assurance-is-here,-standards-are-converging,-and-Scope-3-remains-the-stress-point.html) notes that "Scope 3 disclosures carry more inherent limitations than Scopes 1–2" and auditors are "keeping probing Scope 3 estimates in the next filing cycle."

The competitive map shows that **no incumbent tool solves the primary-data problem at product level**:

| Platform | Corporate GHG | Product LCA | Primary data | Audit trail | Auditor-accepted |
|----------|--------------|-------------|--------------|-------------|-----------------|
| Mondra | No | Yes (UK food) | Partial (improving) | Limited | Emerging |
| Watershed + HowGood | Yes | Yes (food) | Secondary dominant | Yes | Yes (enterprise) |
| Manufacture 2030 | Partial | No | Supplier-reported | Limited | Not standalone |
| Anthesis PortfolioPro | Yes | Yes | Secondary | Consulting-grade | Yes (with review) |
| ERM CVS | N/A | N/A | N/A | Independent verification | Yes (accredited) |
| **MGS** | Yes (via platform) | **Yes (primary process data)** | **Yes (mass energy balance)** | **Design goal** | **Should be designed to be** |

**The "alongside then replace" model works because:**
1. Initial engagement: MGS provides the primary process data that consultants (Anthesis, ERM) are trying to extract through supplier engagement programmes
2. As data improves, the consultant's value-add (interpretation, strategy) remains but the data collection/calculation becomes MGS's domain
3. Auditors increasingly require primary data over secondary — this structurally disadvantages secondary-data platforms and consultants who rely on them
4. ISSA 5000 (effective Dec 2026) introduces enhanced value chain guidance — product-level primary data will become explicitly important for assurance engagements on food retailer Scope 3

**Key relationships to target first:**
- Morrisons (mid-maturity; explicit need to improve Scope 3 accuracy; M2030 embedded but no product LCA)
- M&S food suppliers (M&S is explicitly asking Mondra for primary supplier data — those suppliers need to produce it)
- Unilever's 300-supplier programme (suppliers need PCF capability; Ecochain + Nexio is the current tool — MGS could be an alternative)
- Ingredient manufacturers serving UK food retail (Myton Food Group, Arla, Müller — already reported primary data to Morrisons)
