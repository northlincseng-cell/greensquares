# Verification, Traceability & Anti-Greenwashing for My Green Squares (MGS)
## Deep Research Report — March 2026

---

## Executive Summary

The relaunched MGS operates at a scale — 100g CO₂e per Green Square, priced at £0.003 — that has no direct precedent in established carbon markets. Every tonne-based standard, every registry, and every blockchain protocol was designed for 1,000kg units, not 100g units. This creates both a challenge and an opportunity: MGS must *bridge* certified, tonne-scale projects to consumer-facing micro-credits, and must do so in a way that is legally defensible, independently verifiable, and invulnerable to the greenwashing accusations that collapsed the original MGS market in 2013–2015.

The greenwashing scandals of that era were not primarily about small operators like MGS — they were about systematic over-crediting of baseline deforestation scenarios in large REDD+ projects (some studies found up to 94% of credits were "phantom"), combined with misleading "carbon neutral" marketing by major corporates. MGS was collateral damage. The relaunch can position itself as the *antithesis* of that era by embedding verification and traceability into the product architecture from day one.

This report provides the evidence base for that architecture.

## 1. VERIFICATION STANDARDS

### 1.1 Verra VCS — The World's Largest Carbon Crediting Programme

The Verified Carbon Standard (VCS) Programme, administered by [Verra](https://verra.org/programs/verified-carbon-standard/), is the world's most widely used GHG crediting programme. Every Verified Carbon Unit (VCU) represents one metric tonne of CO₂e reduced or removed from the atmosphere.

**Rigour of the VCS verification process:**

VCS operates a two-stage independent audit system:

| Stage | What Happens | Who Does It |
|-------|-------------|-------------|
| **Validation** | Determines whether a project meets all VCS rules and requirements before credits are issued. Review of Project Description Document and monitoring methodology | Approved Validation/Verification Body (VVB) |
| **Verification** | Confirms that the outcomes described in project documentation have actually been achieved and quantified correctly | Independent VVB (different from validator in many cases) |

VVBs must be:
- Accredited by an International Accreditation Forum (IAF) member under the Multilateral Recognition Arrangement (MLA)
- Specifically approved by Verra with a signed agreement
- Holding accreditation for the relevant sectoral scope

**Verra's internal oversight layer:**

As of 2024, Verra runs a [VVB Performance Monitoring Programme (PMP)](https://verra.org/validation-verification/) using 17 indicators across four categories:
1. Project Reviews — every audit submission is reviewed internally before credits are issued
2. Performance Observation Audits — Verra staff witness VVBs conducting live audits
3. Sanctions and Cooperation — non-conformance reports, suspensions, terminations
4. Accreditation Body Feedback

VVBs that underperform receive scorecards and must undertake corrective action or risk suspension. Verra has the discretion to terminate VVBs.

**Known weaknesses (important for MGS positioning):**

The January 2023 Guardian/Zeit investigation and associated academic analysis found that a significant percentage of Verra REDD+ credits may have been over-credited due to inflated baseline deforestation scenarios. Under the *pre-2023 methodology*, some projects appeared to overstate deforestation threats by around 400% on average. [Verra has responded](https://verra.org/verra-views/beyond-the-headlines-a-constructive-response-to-criticism-of-validation-and-verification-in-the-vcm/) by noting that only four VVBs were responsible for the majority of flagged projects, and introduced VM0048 — a new REDD+ methodology — in November 2023.

**VM0048: The Reform Methodology (Post-2023)**

Verra's [new REDD methodology VM0048](https://verra.org/program-notice/setting-the-standard-verras-revolutionary-new-redd-methodology/) introduces:
- Baselines calculated using a **single, jurisdiction-wide deforestation dataset** established by Verra — removing the ability for project developers to select their own reference regions
- Verra allocates baseline data to projects based on independently assessed deforestation risk maps
- All AUD (Avoiding Unplanned Deforestation) projects within a jurisdiction must be proportionate to total verified deforestation

**New additionality tools (October 2024):**

Verra has released [VCS Tools VT0008 and VT0009](https://verra.org/verra-releases-new-vcs-additionality-tools/) specifically designed to meet the [ICVCM Core Carbon Principles (CCP) Assessment Framework](https://icvcm.org/core-carbon-principles/) requirements on additionality. Projects using these new tools can apply for the CCP label.

**Implication for MGS:** The VCS standard, when applied to projects using VM0048 and the new additionality tools, is now substantially more robust than the pre-2023 version. The key MGS claim should be: *"The Juma VCS project was registered and issued under the VCS Programme. For the relaunch, MGS will exclusively use projects with CCP-label eligibility or equivalent reformed methodology credentials."*

### 1.2 Gold Standard — The Premium Benchmark

[Gold Standard](https://www.goldstandard.org) (GS) was founded by WWF in 2003 and is widely regarded as the most rigorous voluntary carbon standard for project quality. It is particularly strong on co-benefits (Sustainable Development Goals).

**Verification process summary:**
1. Project Listed — preliminary design document submitted
2. Validation by accredited VVB — site visit and/or desk review against GS4GG Principles & Requirements. Must include SDG impact assessment against at least 3 SDGs (including SDG13 Climate) using the digital SDG Impact Tool. Must include a full Stakeholder Consultation Report
3. **"Gold Standard Design Certified"** status awarded
4. Monitoring period
5. Verification by independent VVB — confirms monitoring data; performance review by Gold Standard itself
6. **"Certified Gold Standard Project"** status and credit issuance

**Paris Agreement alignment (effective January 2026):**

[Gold Standard updated its requirements](https://www.goldstandard.org/publications/certification-process-stepbystep) so all carbon credits with vintages from January 2026 onward must use Paris-aligned rules: stronger additionality demonstration, improved leakage treatment, higher data quality expectations, and reflection of national climate targets in project baselines.

**Gold Standard vs VCS for MGS:**

| Criterion | VCS (post-VM0048) | Gold Standard |
|-----------|------------------|---------------|
| Market scale | Largest by volume | Smaller, premium |
| REDD+ focus | Strong, reformed | Less REDD+ focus |
| SDG co-benefits | Optional (CCB label) | Mandatory (3+ SDGs) |
| Price premium | Standard | 20–40% premium typical |
| CCP compatibility | Yes (largest eligible programme) | Yes |
| Suitable for Juma-type projects | Yes | Less common |

**Recommendation for MGS:** VCS with CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) co-certification is the appropriate standard for conservation/REDD+ projects like Juma. Gold Standard is better suited for renewable energy, clean cookstoves, and other technology-based projects if MGS diversifies its project portfolio.

### 1.3 Plan Vivo — The Community-First Standard

[Plan Vivo](https://www.planvivo.org/projects/certify-a-project/pvclimate) has over 25 years' experience certifying community and smallholder-led nature-based projects. Its PV Climate standard is notable for:

- **Minimum 60% revenue sharing** with communities on the ground — a requirement built into the standard
- **Independent Expert pathway** for microscale projects (generating < 10,000 tCO₂e/year): a viable alternative to full VVBs, using approved individuals with relevant qualifications
- Annual Reports required for credit issuance; third-party verification every 5 years minimum
- All validation and verification reports made publicly available
- Corrective Action Requests (CARs) issued for non-compliance, with defined deadlines

**Plan Vivo's relevance for MGS:**

The microscale pathway (< 10,000 tCO₂e/year) and emphasis on community benefit make Plan Vivo particularly interesting for smaller MGS-linked conservation projects. If MGS builds a portfolio beyond the Juma Reserve (e.g., UK-based projects, smaller community conservation funds), Plan Vivo would be worth considering. However, the standard is less prominent in corporate procurement than VCS/Gold Standard.

### 1.4 ICROA — The Industry Accreditor

The [International Carbon Reduction and Offset Alliance (ICROA)](https://icroa.org) does not certify projects — it **accredits service providers** and **endorses crediting programmes**.

**ICROA Endorsement of Crediting Programmes:**

ICROA's [endorsement criteria](https://icroa.org/standard-endorsement/) assess programmes across 8 categories:
1. Independence
2. Governance
3. Registry
4. Validation/verification
5. Carbon crediting principles
6. Environmental/social impacts
7. Stakeholder considerations
8. Scale

To qualify, a programme must have ≥ 10 projects registered and ≥ 100,000 tCO₂e issued. Both Verra VCS and Gold Standard are ICROA-endorsed. Plan Vivo is also endorsed.

**ICROA Approved Organisation status:**

The [ICROA Code of Best Practice](https://icroa.org/approval/icroa-code-of-best-practice/) governs service providers (brokers, advisors, aggregators). Requirements include:
- Transacting and retiring credits exclusively from ICROA-endorsed crediting programmes
- Implementing comprehensive carbon management strategies for clients (measure → reduce → compensate)
- Annual independent third-party compliance audit

**ICROA relevance for MGS:**

If MGS seeks ICROA Approved Organisation status for its CIC conservation fund, this would be a significant trust signal — the standard that corporate buyers of carbon credits look for when evaluating their offset providers. It would mean an annual independent audit confirming that every Green Square retirement traces to a real VCU from an endorsed programme.

### 1.5 ICVCM Core Carbon Principles — The Global Quality Benchmark

The [Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM)](https://icvcm.org/core-carbon-principles/) has established 10 Core Carbon Principles (CCPs) as the global threshold for credit quality:

| # | Principle | Key Requirement |
|---|-----------|-----------------|
| 1 | Effective governance | Independent board, transparent processes |
| 2 | Tracking | Registry uniquely identifies each credit, no duplication |
| 3 | Transparency | All project info publicly available in accessible formats |
| 4 | Robust independent third-party validation & verification | Programme-level requirements for rigorous auditing |
| 5 | Additionality | Reductions would not have occurred without carbon credit revenues |
| 6 | Permanence | Reductions are permanent or reversal risk is adequately covered |
| 7 | Robust quantification | Conservative, complete, science-based GHG methods |
| 8 | No double-counting | No double issuance, double claiming, or double use |
| 9 | Sustainable development benefits & safeguards | Positive SD impacts; complies with international best practices |
| 10 | Net zero contribution | Does not lock in GHG-intensive technologies incompatible with 2050 targets |

As of 2024, all major crediting programmes (including VCS and Gold Standard) are CCP-Eligible, and first CCP-Approved methodologies were announced in June 2024. Credits with CCP labels carry the highest achievable integrity signal in the voluntary carbon market.

**MGS implication:** Sourcing from CCP-Approved categories is the gold standard for defensible claims. MGS should specify this as a requirement for the project portfolio supporting Green Squares.

### 1.6 What "Independently Audited" Means for MGS

"Independently audited" for MGS would need to operate at two levels:

**Level 1 — Project audit (already in place via VCS/Gold Standard):**
- The underlying tonne-scale carbon credits (VCUs) are independently verified by accredited VVBs against the relevant standard
- All verification reports are publicly accessible on the Verra Registry
- Verra's own staff conduct additional internal review before credits are issued

**Level 2 — Micro-credit aggregation audit (MGS-specific):**
- An independent auditor (suggested: a mid-tier UK accounting firm with sustainability practice, or specialist sustainability assurance provider) confirms that:
  - Each Green Square sold corresponds to exactly 100g CO₂e in VCUs held or retired
  - The ratio of Green Squares issued to VCUs acquired is 10,000:1 (10,000 × 100g = 1 tonne)
  - No Green Square is issued more than once
  - VCU retirements on the Verra Registry match the cumulative number of Green Squares sold
- Annual publication of a Verification Statement confirming the above

This two-level structure — project-level VCS audit + aggregation-level independent audit — would be substantially more rigorous than anything claimed by the bad actors in the 2013–2015 scandal.

## 2. BLOCKCHAIN TRACEABILITY

### 2.1 How Blockchain Provides Immutable Traceability

Blockchain technology provides three key properties that address the core failure modes of carbon markets:

| Property | How It Works | What It Solves |
|----------|-------------|----------------|
| **Immutability** | Once a transaction is recorded on-chain, it cannot be altered. Every credit issuance, transfer, and retirement is time-stamped and locked | Prevents retroactive manipulation of records |
| **Unique tokenisation** | Each credit is represented as a unique token (often NFT) with metadata including project ID, vintage, serial number, retirement status | Makes duplication technically impossible |
| **Automated retirement** | Smart contracts automatically mark a credit as retired when it is purchased, locking it permanently | Eliminates human error and fraud in retirement process |

As [blockchain analysts note](https://techaroha.com/blockchain-prevents-double-counting-carbon-credits/), studies suggest double-counting could distort up to 30–40% of claimed global carbon offsets. Blockchain makes this structurally impossible once credits are on-chain, because:
- Off-chain originals are locked when bridged to blockchain
- The same token cannot exist twice on a decentralised ledger
- Retirement is automated and irreversible

**Important caveat:** Blockchain ensures *record integrity*, not *environmental validity*. External auditors still must verify that the underlying emission reductions are real before data goes on-chain. As researchers note: "blockchain ensures transparency, while verification ensures quality."

Smart contracts can automate the entire MGS lifecycle:

```

        ↓
Smart contract triggered
        ↓
Corresponding fraction of VCU locked in registry account
```

The [new Verra registry](https://verra.org/verra-registry-transition/) (developed with S&P Global Commodity Insights, announced August 2025) will feature "transaction-ready APIs that allow for automated transfers and retirements of units, replacing manual processes and enabling frictionless, high-volume trading across brokers, exchanges, and marketplaces." This is the infrastructure MGS needs: direct API integration to auto-retire VCUs at point of sale.

### 2.4 Preventing Double-Counting at the Micro Level

The double-counting challenge for MGS is specific: one VCU (1 tonne) must correspond to exactly 10,000 Green Squares (10,000 × 100g). The prevention architecture:

**Layer 1 — Registry level:** VCUs are retired on the Verra Registry under a single account controlled by the MGS CIC conservation fund. Once retired, a VCU serial number cannot be re-used. This is enforced by Verra's registry infrastructure.

**Layer 2 — Aggregation ledger:** A blockchain (or secure database with audit trail) maps each batch of VCU retirements to exactly 10,000 Green Square issuances. This can be implemented via:
- A private/consortium blockchain maintained by MGS and its auditor
- A public blockchain (Ethereum L2, Polygon, or similar low-cost chain) where Green Square issuances are recorded as tokens linked to VCU serial numbers

**Layer 3 — Smart contract logic:** The smart contract enforces the 10,000:1 ratio — it cannot mint more Green Squares than the VCU inventory (held but not yet retired) supports.

**Layer 4 — Public verification portal:** Consumers can query their Green Square serial number against the blockchain ledger and confirm it traces to a specific VCU retirement on the Verra Registry.

### 2.5 Cost Considerations — Is Blockchain Viable at £0.003 per Unit?

This is the critical practical question. The answer depends entirely on the blockchain architecture chosen.

**Why public L1 chains (Ethereum mainnet) are not viable:**

Ethereum mainnet gas fees typically run $0.50–$5.00+ per transaction depending on network congestion. Recording each individual Green Square retirement on-chain would cost more than the credit itself. This is not viable.

**Why private/consortium blockchain is viable:**

Enterprise blockchain solutions (permissioned chains, Layer 2 rollups, or institutional platforms like Kinexys) operate with dramatically lower transaction costs:

| Architecture | Approx. Cost per Transaction | Suitable for MGS? |
|-------------|-----------------------------|--------------------|
| Ethereum mainnet | $0.50 – $5.00 | No |
| Ethereum L2 (Polygon, Arbitrum) | $0.001 – $0.01 | Potentially |
| Private/consortium blockchain | < $0.001 | Yes |
| Batch processing (record batches, not individual credits) | Fraction of per-unit cost | Yes — optimal |

**The optimal architecture for MGS:**

Batch processing combined with individual lookup is the practical solution:
1. Green Square sales are recorded in a secure off-chain database (or consortium chain) in real-time
2. At defined intervals (e.g., daily), a batch of VCU retirements is executed via the Verra Registry API, corresponding to accumulated Green Square sales
3. Each batch retirement is anchored on-chain with a hash — the hash links to the list of Green Square IDs it covers
4. Consumers can verify their individual Green Square ID within the batch using a lookup tool

This architecture makes the per-unit blockchain cost effectively zero for individual credits while preserving full auditability. Research confirms that "only limited operations need to be recorded on-chain (issuance, transfer, retirement) while maintaining efficiency."

**Supporting evidence on cost reduction:** A [2024 PwC report on carbon credit tokenisation](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC12924408/) and academic analysis confirm that tokenisation can achieve up to 85% cost reduction in clearing and settlement activities by 2028 compared to traditional infrastructure.

**Conclusion:** Blockchain is viable for MGS at the £0.003 price point *if* implemented using batch processing and/or enterprise/consortium infrastructure. Individual per-credit on-chain recording at L1 costs is not viable at this price level. This distinction should be clearly communicated to avoid over-promising on the technical architecture.

## 3. CIC (Community Interest Company) STRUCTURE

### 3.1 Why a CIC for the Conservation Fund

A Community Interest Company (CIC) is a limited company that exists to benefit the community. It was introduced by the UK government in 2005 and sits between an ordinary trading company and a full charity.

**The fundamental difference that matters for MGS:**

The [Asset Lock](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/community-interest-companies-how-to-form-a-cic/community-interest-companies-guidance-chapters) is the defining legal feature of a CIC. It is:
- **Statutory and irremovable** — it cannot be taken out of the CIC's Articles of Association
- **Prevents private gain** — no assets can be transferred from the CIC to an ordinary limited company or to individuals (directors, members, or shareholders)
- **Permanent** — survives any change of management or ownership

This means that revenue flowing into the MGS CIC conservation fund *cannot* be extracted for private benefit. Every pound spent on Green Squares that flows to the conservation fund is legally locked for community/environmental purposes.

### 3.2 Legal Protections and Asset Lock

The [CIC asset lock](https://www.harpermacleod.co.uk/insights/the-asset-lock-is-a-cic-the-right-structure-for-your-social-enterprise/) means:
- Assets must either be **retained within the CIC** for community purposes, OR
- Transferred to another **asset-locked body** (another CIC, a registered charity, a community benefit society, or a non-UK equivalent)
- Transferred for **full market value** (preserving asset value within the CIC)
- Transferred to another asset-locked body **with CIC Regulator consent**

This provides multiple layers of legal protection:
1. The CIC Regulator (part of Companies House) has oversight of the company
2. The asset lock is embedded in the Articles of Association — it requires Companies Act procedures to change (practically impossible without Regulator approval)
3. Annual CIC Report filed with Companies House confirms community benefit activities

**Limited dividends are possible:** A CIC limited by shares can distribute up to a capped dividend (currently capped by the CIC Regulator) — but the community must remain the primary beneficiary.

### 3.3 How CIC Status Builds Consumer Trust

For consumer-facing communications, the CIC structure provides:

1. **Demonstrable legal commitment** — not just a policy statement, but a legally enforced structure. "Our conservation fund is a CIC, which means by law the assets cannot be used for private profit."

2. **Regulatory oversight** — the CIC Regulator can investigate complaints and enforce compliance. This is verifiable by consumers.

3. **Transparency requirements** — annual CIC Report is filed publicly at Companies House, available for scrutiny

4. **Differentiation from past greenwashing actors** — many of the bad actors in 2013–2015 were ordinary limited companies with no legal constraints on how offset revenues were used. A CIC structure materially changes this.

5. **Trust shorthand** — consumers familiar with the social enterprise sector recognise CIC as a credible legal form. It signals mission-lock in a way that voluntary pledges cannot.

### 3.4 Examples of Successful Environmental CICs in the UK

Several UK environmental organisations have adopted the CIC structure:

- **9Trees CIC** ([9trees.org](https://www.9trees.org)) — UK tree planting organisation structured as a CIC, using the model "9 trees is the number required to balance the average UK citizen's carbon consumption each year." Provides a direct precedent for carbon-related CIC activity.

- **Amity CIC** — featured in Manchester's Zero Carbon programme (Manchester Climate Change Agency partnership), demonstrating that CICs can be integrated into large-scale urban climate action programmes.

- Multiple nature recovery and peatland restoration projects receiving UK government Landscape Recovery funding have adopted CIC or community benefit structures.

**Key insight:** The CIC model is now well-established in the UK environmental and social enterprise sector. The legal infrastructure, Companies House filing requirements, and regulatory oversight are mature. MGS's use of a CIC for its conservation fund follows an established and understood pattern.

## 4. ANTI-GREENWASHING FRAMEWORK

### 4.1 UK Green Claims Code (CMA)

The [Competition and Markets Authority (CMA) Green Claims Code](https://www.gov.uk/government/publications/green-claims-code-making-environmental-claims/environmental-claims-on-goods-and-services) sets out **6 principles** that govern all environmental claims under UK consumer protection law:

| # | Principle | What It Requires for MGS |
|---|-----------|--------------------------|
| 1 | **Truthful and accurate** | Every claim about CO₂e reduction must be factually correct. "Each Green Square represents 100g CO₂e reduction" must be demonstrably true. Cannot overstate or exaggerate. |
| 2 | **Clear and unambiguous** | Avoid technical language without explanation. "Verified Carbon Unit" needs to be explained in plain English. No vague terms like "eco-friendly" or "green" without specifics. |
| 3 | **Must not omit or hide important relevant information** | Must not hide that VCS credits have faced criticism; must not hide that this is one step in a broader climate response, not a complete solution. |
| 4 | **Comparisons must be fair and meaningful** | If comparing to competitors, must use identical methodology and data. Comparative claims ("more verifiable than...") require substantiation. |
| 5 | **Must consider the full life cycle** | For a carbon credit product, this means the full chain from forest activity through to Green Square retirement. Must consider permanence risks (e.g., deforestation reversal). |
| 6 | **Must be substantiated** | Up-to-date, credible evidence must exist *before* any claim is made. Cannot publish and then gather evidence. Evidence must be accessible. |

**Enforcement teeth (since April 2025):**

Under the [Digital Markets, Competition and Consumers Act (DMCCA) 2024](https://cse-net.org/uk-greenwashing-rules-2025-compliance-guide/), the CMA can:
- Directly fine companies up to **10% of global turnover** for misleading environmental claims — without going to court
- Issue enforcement orders requiring immediate removal of claims
- These powers have been active since **6 April 2025**

The [January 2026 CMA supply chain guidance](https://www.tlt.com/insights-and-events/insight/navigating-green-claims-cma-guidance-for-supply-chain-actors) additionally establishes that liability is **shared but differentiated** across supply chains — meaning a retailer selling Green Squares bears responsibility for the accuracy of claims even if those claims originate from MGS.

**The FCA anti-greenwashing rule** (effective 31 May 2024) applies to any FCA-authorised firms making sustainability claims — relevant if MGS or its retail partners are regulated by the FCA.

### 4.2 EU Green Claims Directive (2023/2024)

The [EU Green Claims Directive](https://environment.ec.europa.eu/topics/circular-economy-topics/green-claims_en) (proposed March 2023, progressing through EU legislative process) requires that for any environmental claim:

1. Claims must be **backed by science-based proof** adhering to specific requirements
2. Companies must **specify what the claim covers** (whole company, specific product, part of a product)
3. Claims must show **significant benefit on the product life-cycle**
4. Claims must **not ignore significant trade-offs** (e.g., a claim about carbon that worsens land use cannot be made without disclosing the land-use impact)
5. Claims must **go beyond legal compliance** — you cannot claim environmental benefit for something you're required to do by law
6. For **carbon offset claims specifically**: Must clearly explain whether offsets represent emission reductions or removals, and how they are achieved
7. Environmental labels and claims must be **certified by an accredited body** before use, with an EU-wide certificate of conformity
8. Claims must be **reviewed and updated at least every five years**

**MGS relevance:** Even though MGS is UK-based, the EU Green Claims Directive will affect any MGS activity in EU markets or EU-facing retail partnerships. More importantly, the Directive is shaping international best practice — aligning MGS claims with EU requirements provides substantial protection even in the UK market, where regulators are watching EU standards closely.

**Microenterprise exemption:** The Directive exempts businesses with fewer than 10 employees and turnover below €2 million — potentially relevant for MGS at launch, but not a long-term strategy given growth ambitions.

### 4.3 FTC Green Guides (US)

The [FTC Green Guides](https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/environmental-claims-summary-green-guides) (last formally updated 2012; update proposed but not finalised by 2025 — the proposed revision was withdrawn in 2024) provide non-binding but influential guidance:

**Specific carbon offset requirements:**
- Marketers must have **"competent and reliable scientific evidence"** to support carbon offset claims
- Must use **appropriate accounting methods** that measure emission reductions correctly and **do not sell credits more than once** (anti-double-counting requirement)
- **Must disclose** if the emission reductions being offset will **not occur for at least two years** (the "two-year rule")
- **Deceptive** to claim a carbon offset if the activity generating the offset is **already required by law**

The parallel guidance in Canada and EU is now in force and serves as de facto US benchmark even without FTC update.

**MGS FTC implication:** The Juma Reserve's REDD activities are not legally required — they are *additional* (see Section 6). The two-year rule is satisfied because VCS verification cycles are conducted periodically but credits represent *already-achieved* reductions at the time of issuance. MGS should explicitly state vintage years on its Green Square certificates.

### 4.5 Structuring Communications for Legal Defensibility

Based on the Green Claims Code, EU Directive, and FTC Guides, MGS should apply the following communication architecture:

**Claim hierarchy:**

```
LAYER 1 — SPECIFIC VERIFIED FACT (always lead with this)
"Each Green Square = 100g CO₂e, verified under Verra VCS"

LAYER 2 — MECHANISM EXPLANATION (always accompany)

LAYER 4 — SUBSTANTIATION LINK (always accessible)
"Verify your Green Square: [URL]"
```

**Documentation requirements:**
- Keep a live evidence file with current verification reports, VCU retirement records, and CIC accounts
- Date-stamp all claims; set calendar reminders for 5-year review (EU Directive requirement)
- Do not make forward-looking claims without explicit qualification ("we plan to..." not "we will...")
- Maintain internal approval process for all environmental marketing copy

### 4.6 Lessons from Greenwashing Enforcement Actions

**Shell (ASA, 2025):**
The [ASA ruled in favour of Shell](https://inquisitiveminds.bristows.com/post/102ltxj/asa-rules-in-favour-of-shell-again-on-green-claims) in two 2025 cases, suggesting a more permissive approach to context-specific environmental claims. The key finding: claims understood as case studies of specific decarbonisation work (not representations of the whole company's transition) are less likely to mislead. Lesson for MGS: be specific about what each claim applies to — Green Squares are "a verified contribution to CO₂ reduction" not "making your business carbon neutral."

**M&S "carbon neutral denim" (ASA, previous ruling):**
Found misleading because the claim implied the entire product was carbon neutral when only certain production stages were offset. Lesson: claims must specify exactly what is being offset.

**The 2023 VCM scandal (Guardian investigation):**
Not a regulatory enforcement per se, but the market collapse in reputation for REDD+ credits. Lesson: do not rely solely on third-party certification as the defence. MGS's architecture of *additional* independent audit + blockchain traceability + CIC structure + consumer verification portal is specifically designed to go beyond what those projects offered.

**Volkswagen/Ecosia (UK, CMA investigation):**
CMA investigated "green" claims by automotive companies that could not be substantiated across the vehicle's lifecycle. Lesson: claims about carbon products must consider the full chain.

## 5. REAL-TIME VERIFICATION

### 5.1 Verifying at Point of Sale

The ideal MGS point-of-sale verification flow:

```
Consumer purchases Green Squares
        ↓
MGS system checks current VCU balance in registry account
        ↓
Confirms inventory covers the new Green Squares
        ↓
New Green Squares assigned unique IDs linked to VCU serial numbers
        ↓
```

This can be implemented via the [Verra Registry's new API](https://verra.org/verra-registry-transition/) (transaction-ready APIs being built in the S&P Global partnership, announced August 2025 with details promised Q1 2026) or via intermediary platforms that already bridge Verra data.

**Existing API integration services:**
- [ShiftCarbon Carbon API](https://shiftcarbon.io/solutions/point-of-sale-integration) — "point of sale integration" that allows developers to integrate carbon offsets directly into checkout flows, with listing and verification capabilities
- [Carbonmark](https://www.carbonmark.com/post/carbon-retirement-verification) — provides proof of carbon credit retirement with direct links to Verra Registry records and a "VIEW CARBON PROVENANCE" flow

MGS does not need to build from scratch — white-labelling or integrating existing carbon API infrastructure is the pragmatic approach for launch.

## 6. THE ADDITIONALITY QUESTION

### 6.1 What Additionality Means for MGS

**The core principle (ICVCM CCP #5):**

"The GHG emission reductions or removals from the mitigation activity shall be additional, i.e., they would not have occurred in the absence of the incentive created by carbon credit revenues."

For MGS, additionality operates at two levels:
1. **Project-level additionality:** Would the Juma Reserve forest still be standing without the REDD+ project?
2. **Revenue-level additionality:** Do Green Square purchases provide the marginal financial incentive that keeps the project viable?

### 6.2 VCS-1566 — The Juma Sustainable Development Reserve

**Project overview (from [REDD Projects Database](https://www.reddprojectsdatabase.org/227-the-juma-sustainable-development-reserve-project/) and [Carbonmark](https://app.carbonmark.com/projects/VCS-1566)):**

| Parameter | Detail |
|-----------|--------|
| Location | State of Amazonas, Municipality of Novo Aripuana, Brazil |
| Size | 540,444.5 hectares |
| Project type | REDD (Reducing Emissions from Deforestation and Forest Degradation) |
| Activity | Avoiding Unplanned Deforestation and Degradation (AUDD) |
| Deforestation drivers | Illegal logging, infrastructure, local livelihoods |
| Carbon pools | Above ground biomass, below ground biomass, dead wood |
| Total credits | ~2,385,296 tCO₂e issued |
| Crediting period | 2016–2116 (100 years) |
| Reference period | 2009–2015 |
| Standards | VCS + CCB (Climate, Community & Biodiversity) |
| Project status | Ongoing as of 2022 |
| Implementing body | Fundação Amazonas Sustentável (FAS) — an NGO/private non-profit |

**Note on VCS registration status:** The database (last updated 2020) lists the VCS status as "in process." This is a common state for projects undergoing the new VM0048 methodology transition. MGS should verify current registry status directly with Verra or FAS before launch.

**CCB co-certification:** The project holds Climate, Community & Biodiversity (CCB) certification, which independently verifies positive outcomes for biodiversity and local communities beyond the carbon reductions. This is a significant trust signal — it means the project has been assessed against social and environmental safeguards, not just carbon accounting.

---

### 6.3 How the Juma Reserve Demonstrates Additionality

The Juma Reserve sits in the State of Amazonas — an area under documented deforestation pressure from illegal logging and land conversion for agriculture. Additionality is demonstrated through:

**1. Financial viability analysis:**

REDD projects must demonstrate that forest conservation is *not* financially viable without carbon credit revenues. In the Amazon context, deforestation for agriculture (cattle ranching, soy) has clear short-term financial returns. The REDD project shows carbon credit revenue is the decisive factor that makes conservation economically superior to conversion.

**2. Common practice analysis:**

The project must show that forest conservation without payment is *not* common practice in the region. Given that the broader Amazonas region faces ongoing deforestation pressure, this is demonstrable.

**3. Barrier analysis (REDD-specific):**

Conservation faces structural barriers: illegal logging revenue, infrastructure development, and subsistence agriculture. The barrier analysis documents that carbon finance overcomes these barriers — specifically, that without REDD revenue, the landowners/government would not have the financial means to enforce conservation.

**4. Counterfactual (baseline) scenario:**

Under the pre-2023 methodology, baselines were set by project developers with reference to similar regions — this was the source of the systematic over-crediting identified in 2023. Under [VM0048](https://verra.org/program-notice/setting-the-standard-verras-revolutionary-new-redd-methodology/), Verra now:
- Sets the baseline using a **single jurisdiction-wide deforestation dataset** approved by Verra itself
- Allocates baseline data to projects based on independently assessed deforestation risk
- Removes the ability for project developers to self-select high-baseline reference regions

The Juma Reserve's reference period (2009–2015) predates VM0048. MGS's strongest position is to acknowledge this history transparently and specify that the relaunch portfolio uses projects that either:
(a) Have been re-verified under VM0048 or equivalent reformed methodology, or
(b) Carry a CCP label from ICVCM, or
(c) Have an independent Sylvera or BeZero rating of B or above

---

### 6.4 The 77% Additionality Finding for Brazilian Amazon REDD

A September 2025 [Climate Policy Initiative study](https://www.climatepolicyinitiative.org/publication/carbon-credits-in-the-amazon-new-methodology-for-assessing-project-additionality/) found that **77% of carbon credits from REDD projects in the Brazilian Amazon are additional** — meaning 23% of conserved forest areas would have remained protected without the projects' incentives.

This provides important context for MGS's messaging:
- REDD in the Amazon is *substantially* additional — the majority of credits represent genuine avoided deforestation
- Not all credits are perfect, which is why the new VM0048 methodology and independent ratings exist
- The Juma Reserve, being in a high-pressure deforestation zone (State of Amazonas, under documented land-use pressure), is in a category more likely to be in the additional 77%

**BeZero Carbon additionality ratings:**

[BeZero Carbon](https://bezerocarbon.com/insights/an-introduction-to-additionality-in-carbon-projects) provides independent third-party additionality ratings for carbon credit projects. Their approach goes beyond the binary pass/fail of standards bodies to assess the *degree* of additionality and the robustness of the evidence. An MGS commitment to only use projects with a BeZero rating of B or above (or equivalent Sylvera rating) would be a powerful differentiator.

---

### 6.5 Additionality of the MGS Micro-Credit Concept Itself

There is a meta-additionality question: do consumer micro-credits actually mobilise new conservation finance that would not otherwise flow?

**The affirmative case:**
- The 100g CO₂e / £0.003 price point is accessible to millions of consumers who would never enter the institutional carbon market
- Aggregate demand at micro-scale represents *new money* — a consumer spending £0.30 in a convenience transaction is not displacing their corporate credit purchase
- The CIC structure locks this new money into conservation — it cannot be diverted

**The challenge:**
- Individual consumers have no obligation to reduce first before compensating — unlike the ICROA hierarchy (reduce → compensate). MGS should not imply that purchasing Green Squares replaces other climate action.
- The language should be: "Green Squares are your verified contribution to global carbon reduction — alongside, not instead of, reducing your own footprint."
