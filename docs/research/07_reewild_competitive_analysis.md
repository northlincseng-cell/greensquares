## Executive Summary

Reewild (trading as PlanetPoints, now rebranding consumer-facing product to **Vela**) is a UK-based climate-fintech startup founded September 2020 under the legal entity Reeset.Earth Limited. It has built a behaviour-change loyalty platform that rewards consumers for purchasing lower-carbon products, demonstrated by a high-profile Mastercard-backed UCL pilot study showing an 18.8% reduction in hot meal emissions. The company represents a genuine competitor to MGS in the broad sense of applying loyalty mechanics to decarbonise consumer purchasing — but the two businesses differ sharply in carbon architecture, commercial target, regulatory positioning, and depth of trust infrastructure. Reewild is primarily a *consumer behaviour-change app*; MGS is a *verified decarbonisation instrument* with statutory governance. These are meaningfully different propositions.

## 2. Product Model: How PlanetPoints / Vela Works

### Core Mechanic
PlanetPoints (the scheme name used in the UCL pilot) / Vela points (the evolving consumer brand) are a behaviour-change loyalty currency. The system works as follows:

1. **Earn:** Consumer earns points when they purchase lower-carbon / healthier products at participating outlets, OR by scanning any retail receipt, OR by linking a bank card (card-linked loyalty, in beta). Points are calibrated inversely to carbon footprint — lower-carbon products earn *more* points.
2. **Track:** The Reewild app shows product-level carbon footprint data drawn from a database of 1.2 million products. Users can see a "carbon calorie" breakdown of their basket.
3. **Redeem:** Points are exchanged for commercial vouchers and experiences — Uber, Uber Eats, Deliveroo, Airbnb, OpenTable, John Lewis, Ticketmaster, SpaBreaks, Eurostar, Forest e-bikes, free meals, pizza lottery, UEFA Champions League tickets.
4. **Gamify:** Tier progression, streaks, daily spins, referral bonuses, leaderboards.

### Unit of Value
- **PlanetPoints** — not pegged to a precise CO2e weight in public-facing materials. Points earned correlate with carbon rating tier of the product (e.g., Very Low = 3 pts/£, Low Carbon = 2 pts/£, Medium = 1 pt/£ at UCL; High and Very High Carbon earn 0 points).
- **Carbon Calories (CC)** — the internal carbon unit used in the app's tracking function. 1 CC = 1g CO2e. This is an *informational display unit*, not the loyalty currency itself. Products are rated A–E (traffic light system) based on lifecycle emissions.
- The two units serve different purposes: Carbon Calories educate; PlanetPoints reward. There is no direct, transparent conversion between grammes of CO2 avoided and points earned in the way MGS has a defined 1 Green Square = 100g CO2e peg.

Sources: [UCL Sustainable news, Feb 2025](https://www.ucl.ac.uk/sustainable/news/2025/feb/earn-rewards-sustainable-choices-campus-reewilds-new-planetpoints-loyalty-programme), [Mastercard Reewild article Nov 2024](https://www.mastercard.com/us/en/news-and-trends/stories/2024/rewarding-behavior-moving-more-sustainable-grocery-picks-up-the-food-chain.html), [Vela website](https://www.velarewards.com), [Incredibusy profile](https://incredibusy.com/reewild-track-your-carbon-footprint/)

### How Points Are Earned — Mechanics
| Mechanism | Detail |
|---|---|
| **In-store at partnered venues (POS-integrated)** | Technology plugs directly into checkout POS; itemised transaction data matched to carbon emissions data in real-time. Used in the UCL pilot via CH&CO / Compass Group. |
| **Receipt scanning (any retailer)** | Consumer scans paper receipt via app camera; OCR processes SKU data and assigns points. Universal — works with any UK retailer. No EPOS integration required. |
| **Card-linked (beta)** | Consumer links bank card once; earns points automatically when shopping at partnered retailers. Currently in beta on Vela. Earns 2× points vs. receipt scan. |
| **Online checkout (e-receipts)** | Reewild plugs into Shopify checkout; carbon e-receipt auto-sent post-purchase; consumer redeems on app. Used by TENZING and Good Food People. |

### What Consumers Do With Points
Points redeem against a commercial rewards portfolio that is explicitly *aspirational* — it emphasises lifestyle upgrades (travel, dining, events) rather than conservation projects. There is no consumer-directed conservation fund or offset component analogous to MGS. The rewards are entirely commercial/hedonic.

### Is There a Conservation/Offset Component?
- **Minimal and indirect.** The Shopify B2B plugin enables brands to plant a tree per order — this is a brand-facing carbon action, not a consumer-directed conservation mechanism. There is no consumer choice over which conservation project to support.
- **No nature reserve / conservation fund infrastructure** comparable to MGS's CIC.
- Reewild's earlier brand identity featured "Reewilders... invest in certified reforestation projects" but this positioning has been largely de-emphasised in favour of the commercial loyalty/rewards framing.

### Is There a Transition/Decarbonisation Component?
- **No.** Reewild does not distinguish between offsets and transition action. The system encourages substitution of lower-carbon products within the existing consumption basket (e.g., plant-based vs. meat) but does not underpin verified real-economy decarbonisation, supply chain transition finance, or the corporate decarbonisation infrastructure that characterises MGS.
- The behaviour-change IS the decarbonisation mechanism — a meaningful but fundamentally different theory of change to MGS's verified transition action model.

## 3. The UCL Pilot Study

### Structure and Execution
- **Pilot name:** PlanetPoints (Planet Points) eco-loyalty pilot
- **Location:** Cafes and dining halls across the UCL campus, London
- **Caterer:** CH&CO (owned by Compass Group PLC)
- **Duration:** Six weeks (approximately Feb–July 2025 based on announcement timeline)
- **Sample:** 900 students
- **Funder/partner:** Mastercard (via Start Path / Sustainability Innovation Lab)
- **Academic assessment:** UCL Centre for Behaviour Change; MSc student Ben Rezos (thesis project)
- **Academic endorsement:** Christopher Marquis, Professor at University of Cambridge, participated in London Climate Action Week roundtable on the pilot

### Mechanism During Pilot
- POS system plugged directly into UCL checkout, matching itemised purchase data to carbon emissions data in real-time
- Carbon ratings for products calculated by Food at UCL using the **Foodsteps platform** (Poore & Nemecek 2018 database)
- Points tiered: Very Low carbon = 3 pts/£, Low Carbon = 2 pts/£, Medium Carbon = 1 pt/£, High/Very High Carbon = 0 pts/£
- Points redeemable for: free coffees, vegetarian meals, Forest e-bike rentals, Uber Eats, Street Slice pizza lottery, UEFA Champions League tickets

### Published Results
| Metric | Result |
|---|---|
| Reduction in emissions from hot meals | **↓ 18.8%** |
| Drop in overall basket emissions per user | **↓ 16.8%** |
| Increase in transaction frequency per month | **↑ 9.8%** |
| Lift in average basket value per user | **↑ 5.5%** |
| Behavioural finding | Students intentionally shifted to locations where PlanetPoints were available |

Sources: [Mastercard July 2025](https://www.mastercard.com/us/en/news-and-trends/Insights/2025/planet-points-reewild-sustainable-consumption.html), [Freddie Lintell LinkedIn July 2025](https://www.linkedin.com/posts/freddie-lintell-905064127_behaviouraleconomics-consumerincentives-activity-7353743777674526722-uEyI)

### Where to Find Results
- Mastercard Insights article (July 22, 2025): https://www.mastercard.com/us/en/news-and-trends/Insights/2025/planet-points-reewild-sustainable-consumption.html
- UCL Green Gown Awards nomination (October 2025): https://www.ucl.ac.uk/news/2025/oct/ucl-and-food-ucl-named-finalists-green-gown-awards-sustainable-food-innovation
- London Climate Action Week roundtable (June 2025) — referenced by Christopher Marquis on LinkedIn
- The MSc thesis by Ben Rezos (UCL Centre for Behaviour Change) represents the formal academic write-up, but as an MSc thesis it has not been published in a peer-reviewed journal. This is a significant distinction: the headline numbers are from industry communications, not peer-reviewed academic publications.

### Strengths of the Study
- Directly measured changes in consumer purchasing behaviour at the point of sale using real transaction data
- Large-ish sample for a loyalty pilot (900 participants)
- UCL Centre for Behaviour Change involvement adds academic credibility
- Positive commercial ROI signal: +9.8% transaction frequency and +5.5% basket value = retailer business case

### Limitations and Critical Caveats
1. **Self-selection bias:** Students who downloaded the app to participate were likely already more eco-conscious than the average consumer — the measured emission reductions may overstate what would be achieved with a general population.
2. **Closed, controlled environment:** UCL campus is a captive audience with limited retail choice, pre-existing carbon labelling infrastructure since 2021, a university sustainability policy mandating 65%+ plant-rich food environment, and a strongly pro-sustainability student demographic. Results are unlikely to replicate in a standard UK supermarket or convenience setting.
3. **Six-week duration:** Short enough that initial novelty effects (engagement with a new app, curiosity about points) may inflate behavioural change. Longer-term durability of behaviour change is undemonstrated.
4. **No peer-reviewed publication:** Results communicated via Mastercard marketing content and an MSc thesis, not a peer-reviewed journal. Standards of causal identification have not been independently assessed, though Christopher Marquis notes the pilot was "rigorously designed to identify causal effects" including econometric models and qualitative interviews.
5. **Scope limited to food/drink:** No evidence base for extension to general retail CPG, fashion, fuel, or household goods.
6. **No verification of actual supply chain emissions reduction:** The 18.8% figure measures *consumer choice shift* (moving from high-carbon to low-carbon products within the available menu). It does not represent verified decarbonisation of the supply chain or real-economy GHG reduction that would survive third-party audit for CSRD or SBTi purposes.

## 5. Technology

### Integration Architecture
Reewild / Vela operates a multi-layer integration approach designed to serve different retail contexts without requiring deep EPOS investment:

| Integration Method | Technical Mechanism | Use Case |
|---|---|---|
| **POS direct integration** | Technology plugs into checkout POS systems; itemised transaction data matched in real-time to carbon DB | Closed venue (university, corporate canteen, food service) |
| **Receipt OCR scanning** | Consumer photographs paper receipt; app processes via OCR to extract SKU data | Universal, any retailer, no merchant integration required |
| **Card-linked (Open Banking)** | Consumer links bank card/account; transactions auto-matched to retailer/product DB | Partners only, currently beta, earns 2× points |
| **Shopify plugin** | Embeds in merchant Shopify checkout; triggers carbon e-receipt and tree planting | D2C e-commerce brands |
| **Email receipt parsing** | Consumer connects email accounts; system parses e-receipts from retailers | Vela Plus: up to 5 accounts |

### Carbon Data Infrastructure
- **Database:** LCA-based carbon footprint data for 1.2 million products
- **Primary methodology:** Full lifecycle assessment (LCA) — farm to fork
- **Data sources used in different contexts:**
  - *UCL pilot:* Foodsteps platform (Poore & Nemecek 2018 database) — GHGe ratings applied by Food at UCL caterers to their own product catalogue
  - *App (general product DB):* Reewild's own database, built using full LCA data on carbon footprint of products and ingredients
  - *B2B brand work:* CarbonCloud (used for Good Food People LCA calculations)
- **Carbon labelling scale:** A–E traffic light (Very Low → Very High carbon); aligns with UK and EU carbon labelling conventions
- **Carbon Calorie unit:** 1 CC = 1g CO2e (display only; not a tradable unit)

### Mastercard Partnership — What Mastercard Actually Provides
The Mastercard relationship is a **Start Path startup engagement programme** partnership, not an equity investment or licensing deal. Specifically:

- **Mastercard provides:** Expertise in loyalty programme design; access to its network of 150M+ merchants, 3.5Bn+ cards, thousands of banking partners; introductions to VCs, financial institutions, governments; potential co-innovation on product integrations
- **Mastercard's stated interest:** Using Reewild/PlanetPoints as proof of concept for its own Sustainability Innovation Lab work; Mastercard's Carbon Calculator (launched April 2025) is partly informed by the PlanetPoints pilot learnings
- **What Mastercard does NOT provide (confirmed):** Card network infrastructure for transaction data (Reewild uses Open Banking for card-linking, not Mastercard network access); equity funding; exclusive commercial contract
- Ellen Jackowski (Mastercard CSO) described Reewild as an app that Mastercard "piloted" — this is Mastercard supporting a partner startup, not building or owning the product

### Blockchain / Verification Layer
- **None identified.** Reewild does not appear to use blockchain, distributed ledger, or any cryptographic verification layer.
- Carbon data integrity relies on the Foodsteps/CarbonCloud/proprietary LCA database, not independent immutable verification.
- No regulatory-aligned verification standard (GS Verified Carbon Standard, Gold Standard, etc.) has been identified in connection with any Reewild carbon claims.

## 7. Strengths and Weaknesses of Reewild Relative to MGS

### Reewild Strengths

1. **Proven behaviour change evidence** — The UCL pilot produced concrete, quantified results (18.8% emission reduction, 9.8% transaction uplift) that are commercially legible and have been amplified by Mastercard's global communications platform. This is a powerful proof point for the "behaviour change + commercial ROI" narrative that brands and retailers immediately understand.

2. **Mastercard halo** — Association with Mastercard's Sustainability Innovation Lab gives Reewild outsized institutional credibility for a seed-stage startup. Mastercard's CSO is publicly citing Reewild as the model for Mastercard's own Carbon Calculator development. This creates a powerful distribution amplifier: if Mastercard embeds Reewild/Vela-style functionality into its issuer network, Reewild's technology could scale to billions of cardholders without requiring individual retailer EPOS integrations.

3. **Zero-friction consumer access** — Receipt scanning means any consumer with a smartphone can earn points from any receipt at any retailer, without any retailer needing to integrate anything. This frictionless onboarding could achieve faster consumer acquisition than an EPOS-dependent model.

4. **Health + sustainability dual positioning** — The Vela rebrand explicitly couples healthy choices with sustainable choices as a dual reward trigger. This broadens addressable market beyond eco-committed consumers to the much larger health-conscious mainstream. The "better choices earn more" framing sidesteps the polarising climate debate entirely.

5. **Academic credibility** — Involvement of UCL Centre for Behaviour Change and a roundtable moderated by a Cambridge professor gives the pilot academic legitimacy that most loyalty startups lack.

6. **App engagement mechanics** — Gamification (streaks, tiers, spins, raffles, Champions League tickets) creates intrinsic engagement loops. The aspirational rewards portfolio (Airbnb, Eurostar, SpaBreaks) positions sustainability as access to life upgrades, not sacrifice — psychologically superior consumer framing.

7. **Global case studies in APAC** — Good Food People pilot demonstrates non-UK applicability, useful for global investor/brand conversations.

8. **Sector-agnostic data layer** — 1.2 million product database means Reewild is not restricted to food; the model could extend across CPG broadly, mirroring MGS's stated scope.

### Reewild Weaknesses

1. **No verified decarbonisation architecture** — Reewild rewards product substitution (buying a lower-carbon product instead of a higher-carbon one), but does not underpin verified supply chain decarbonisation, transition finance, or real-economy emission reductions that survive third-party carbon accounting audit. This is the central structural weakness: the "carbon savings" exist at the consumer choice level, not at the system level. A brand cannot use a Reewild partnership to substantiate CSRD scope 3 reduction claims.

2. **Carbon unit not audit-ready** — PlanetPoints have no fixed, auditable relationship to a gramme of CO2e. Carbon Calories (1g CO2e) are a display metric, not a transacted unit. This makes Reewild unsuitable for regulatory-grade decarbonisation reporting (CSRD, SBTi, UK Green Claims Code).

3. **No blockchain or immutable verification** — Absence of distributed ledger means no independent auditability of the carbon data or points issuance. In an environment of increasing regulatory scrutiny on greenwashing (UK CMA up to 10% of global turnover), this is a liability.

4. **No statutory governance** — Reeset.Earth Ltd is a standard private company; there is no CIC, B-Corp, or statutory asset lock. The conservation/climate mission is not legally protected.

5. **Consumer app dependency (in non-POS mode)** — Receipt scanning requires consumer behaviour change (photographing receipts). Adoption requires active user acquisition; dropout between download and first scan is a known friction point. Card-linking (which removes this friction) is still in beta.

6. **Pilot environment limitations** — UCL campus is atypically favourable: eco-conscious students, pre-existing carbon labelling, limited retail alternatives, university sustainability mandate. The headline behaviour change numbers are unlikely to replicate in Tesco or Asda. The numbers are marketing-grade, not commercially transferable as a guaranteed outcome.

7. **Tiny user base** — 25,000+ users (Vela website) is a micro-scale consumer platform. MGS's EPOS-embedded model does not require users to have an app; it generates GS at scale passively through normal shopping. Reewild's growth is bounded by app adoption.

8. **Rebrand confusion risk** — Operating simultaneously under Reewild, PlanetPoints, and Vela creates fragmented brand recognition. The pivot away from explicit sustainability language (towards "better choices") may alienate the early eco-activist user base while failing to reach mainstream consumers at scale.

9. **No conservation project direction for consumer** — Consumer loyalty to Reewild/Vela delivers no nature conservation outcome beyond the product substitution effect. There is no meaningful conservation funding mechanism comparable to MGS's CIC-backed project portfolio (Amazon, Borneo, Kenya, Scotland). This limits the emotional depth and long-term loyalty of sustainability-motivated users.

10. **Founder-driven, pre-scale** — Three-person founding team, angel-funded, micro-entity accounts. Execution risk at scale is significant if major retail deployments materialise.

11. **Not EPOS-native** — For mass-market grocery (Tesco, Sainsbury's, etc.), deep EPOS integration is operationally superior: it guarantees point award without consumer action, creates retailer-level data richness, and avoids app dropout. Reewild's POS integration has only been deployed in a single controlled venue (UCL / Compass Group). The receipt-scan fallback is a workaround, not a replacement.

## 8. Strategic Implications for MGS

### Where Reewild Is Strong and MGS Should Learn

**1. The Commercial ROI Narrative**
Reewild's most powerful sales tool is the twin metric: "18.8% emission reduction AND 9.8% more transactions." This "decoupling emissions from revenue growth" framing speaks directly to the CFO/CMO as much as the CSO. MGS should develop equivalent commercial ROI proof points from any early pilots — basket size uplift, transaction frequency, brand switching data — and lead with them alongside the carbon narrative in B2B pitches.

**2. Academic Validation Strategy**
Reewild's UCL Centre for Behaviour Change partnership gave a small pilot outsized media weight. MGS should identify an academic partner to formally evaluate its own behaviour change outcomes at pilot stage, targeting a peer-reviewed publication (not an MSc thesis), which would be structurally superior to Reewild's evidence base.

**3. Health as a Co-Driver**
Vela's move to co-reward healthy and sustainable choices is smart market expansion. If MGS's GS methodology could be paired with a health scoring layer (nutrient density, reformulation) at Tier 2 (brand/product level), it would dramatically broaden appeal and align with HFSS regulatory pressures on food brands.

**4. Gamification and Aspirational Rewards**
The Champions League tickets / Airbnb / Eurostar rewards portfolio positions sustainability as access to life upgrades rather than sacrifice. MGS's conservation project narrative (Amazon, Borneo, Kenya, Scotland) speaks to a different motivation — ecological purpose — which is equally powerful but to a narrower initial demographic. MGS should consider whether a commercial rewards layer could sit alongside the conservation direction mechanic to broaden consumer acquisition.

**5. Mastercard-Scale Distribution**
Reewild's most significant strategic asset is the path Mastercard represents: if Mastercard integrates eco-loyalty into its issuer network, Reewild's data infrastructure could sit behind billions of transactions without any EPOS deal needed. MGS should monitor this development closely and consider whether the JP Morgan Kinexys partnership creates an equivalent financial infrastructure play.

### Where MGS Has Clear Advantages

**1. Regulatory-Grade Architecture**
This is MGS's decisive advantage. CSRD is making scope 3 reporting mandatory for large European companies. The PlanetPoints pilot cannot be used to substantiate CSRD scope 3 reductions. MGS's C2050/UN harmonised, Deimos-verified, blockchain-anchored GS is audit-ready. As regulatory pressure intensifies through 2026–2028, brands will need verified instruments, not marketing-grade loyalty schemes. MGS should systematically build its CSRD compliance narrative as a direct differentiator.

**2. The Transition Action / Offset Architecture**
The 75% transition / 25% offset composition with staged progression to 90% transition is sophisticated, defensible against greenwashing scrutiny, and aligned with science-based targets. Reewild has no equivalent framework. This means MGS's GS is a *real decarbonisation instrument*; Reewild's PlanetPoints are a *behaviour change incentive*. These are different things, and the distinction will become commercially critical as CMA enforcement on green claims intensifies.

**3. Zero Consumer App Dependency for Earning**
EPOS embedding means every checkout generates GS automatically — no app download, no receipt scan, no friction. This is structurally superior at scale. Reewild's receipt-scan model caps growth at app-active users. MGS can operate silently at the infrastructure level and then surface the GS narrative for consumers who opt in, creating a much larger passive base.

**4. CIC and Statutory Asset Lock**
For institutional investors, B2B procurement teams, and increasingly regulators, the CIC structure signals authentic mission commitment in a legally protected way. This is a rare and valuable differentiator in a landscape populated by purpose-washing private companies.

**5. Consumer-Directed Conservation**
The ability to choose which conservation project receives one's GS (Amazon, Borneo, Kenya, Scotland) creates deep emotional ownership, generates shareable content, and builds long-term loyalty that commercial rewards (Uber vouchers) cannot replicate. Conservation project direction is a potent retention mechanic for environmentally motivated consumers.

**6. Two-Tier Architecture + Brand Offset Folding**
The Tier 1 / Tier 2 structure and the ability for brands to fold existing offset programmes into GS (no double-counting) creates network effects and switching costs that Reewild's flat model does not. A brand that has invested in MGS infrastructure has converted its existing offset portfolio into GS — unwinding that integration has costs. This is a defensible moat.

**7. Blockchain Traceability (Kinexys)**
JP Morgan Kinexys blockchain provides immutable, auditable traceability that positions MGS ahead of incoming regulatory requirements for environmental claims verification. Reewild has no equivalent.

### Partnership or Collision Risks

**Collision Risks:**
- **Same EPOS moment:** Both MGS and Reewild seek to operate at the retail checkout moment. A retailer choosing Reewild (via POS integration) as its eco-loyalty mechanism would likely not adopt MGS separately — creating a zero-sum dynamic in early retail client acquisition. This is the most acute competitive risk.
- **Same brand budget:** Both are sold to sustainability/marketing teams at brands as scope 3 / consumer engagement solutions. Budget competition is real even if the products are structurally different.
- **Mastercard as accelerant for Reewild:** If Mastercard scales Reewild/Vela into its issuer network, it could reach retailers before MGS establishes EPOS relationships, creating an embedded competitor at scale.

**Partnership Opportunities:**
- **Complementary positioning:** Reewild/Vela is strong on behaviour change and consumer acquisition; MGS is strong on verified decarbonisation and regulatory compliance. A partnership in which Reewild's consumer-facing app surfaces and directs MGS's Green Squares (rather than its own points) would theoretically combine the strengths of both. Freddie Lintell has himself noted the need for ecosystem-level change requiring multiple actors.
- **Academic co-validation:** Both would benefit from shared academic credibility — though direct collaboration risks strengthening a potential competitor.
- **Risk:** Any partnership conversation puts MGS's IP and methodology into Reewild's (and Mastercard's) hands. Approach with caution.

### How MGS Should Position Against Reewild in Pitch Decks

**Against B2B clients (retailers / brands):**
> *"PlanetPoints/Vela demonstrates that consumers want sustainability rewards — the UCL pilot proved demand exists. But PlanetPoints earns you consumer engagement. MGS earns you CSRD compliance. When your scope 3 auditors arrive, a Reewild pilot won't be in your disclosure. A Green Square will."*

**Against institutional investors:**
> *"Reewild is a loyalty app with a carbon story. MGS is a carbon instrument with a loyalty layer. The distinction matters as green claims regulation tightens: Reewild operates in the marketing space, MGS operates in the compliance infrastructure space. Different risk profiles, different regulatory moats, different long-term value."*

**Against conservation/mission investors:**
> *"Reewild rewards consumers with Uber vouchers. MGS directs consumers to name a hectare in the Amazon. The emotional depth, retention, and authentic mission alignment are in a different category."*

**Against sceptics on behaviour change:**
> *"The UCL pilot shows behaviour change is achievable when information and incentive are aligned — we agree. The difference is what that behaviour change is backed by: Reewild backs it with a product database. MGS backs it with verified transition action, blockchain auditability, and a CIC conservation fund. The first is a marketing mechanism; the second is an infrastructure asset."*

**Net assessment:** Reewild has a consumer-facing and marketing advantage today, primarily through the UCL pilot's headline numbers and the Mastercard association. MGS has a structural, regulatory, and trust architecture advantage that becomes decisively more valuable as CSRD enforcement, CMA green claims regulation, and SBTi scope 3 requirements tighten through 2026–2028. The competitive threat from Reewild is most acute in early-mover retail client acquisition; the long-term structural advantage lies firmly with MGS once the regulatory environment matures.
