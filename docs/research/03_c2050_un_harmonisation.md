## Executive Summary

The global carbon market stands at a structural inflection point. The UN has issued its first-ever credits under the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (Article 6.4) in February 2026, signalling the transition from rulemaking to live operation. Simultaneously, JP Morgan's Kinexys blockchain unit is testing tokenisation of carbon credits at the registry level with S&P Global, directly addressing the fragmentation that has historically undermined market credibility. C2050 (Climate Compliance 2050), an Ireland-registered startup, is positioning itself within this environment as a SaaS-based digital monitoring, reporting and verification (D-MRV) platform for evaluating and certifying carbon credits as high-quality, tradable financial assets.

For MGS and its micro-unit Green Square framework (100g CO₂e per unit), the conditions are crystallising into something significant: blockchain infrastructure capable of sub-tonne fractionalization, a UN mechanism that explicitly seeks to include rather than replace existing systems, and institutional players standardising the global price and quality framework. The key risk remains C2050's limited public footprint — the claimed contracts with JP Morgan, KPMG, and S&P are not independently verifiable from public sources as of March 2026, and the company has only a nascent LinkedIn presence with 15 followers.

## Section 2: JP Morgan Blockchain (Kinexys) and Carbon Markets

### Background: Kinexys (formerly Onyx)

JP Morgan's blockchain business unit, **Kinexys** (formerly called Onyx, renamed 2024–2025), was founded in 2015. As of mid-2025, it has processed over **$1.5 trillion in transactions** across more than 10 asset types and is described as a "global provider of blockchain-based infrastructure for the movement of money, assets and information." It operates on the **Kinexys Digital Assets (KDA)** platform, a multi-asset tokenisation infrastructure.

### The Carbon Credit Tokenisation Initiative

On **2 July 2025**, Kinexys announced a new blockchain application specifically for carbon markets, with three testing partners:
- **S&P Global Commodity Insights** (Environmental Registry)
- **EcoRegistry** (blockchain-native carbon registry)
- **International Carbon Registry (ICR)**

This is confirmed by [JP Morgan's official announcement](https://www.jpmorgan.com/payments/newsroom/kda-blockchain-carbon-market-tokenization) and [S&P Global's press release](https://www.spglobal.com/en/press/press-release/sp-global-commodity-insights-exploratory-testing-with-jp-morgan) of the same date.

EcoRegistry and ICR have completed initial integration testing. S&P Global is in exploratory testing phase, with potential future inclusion of its **Meta Registry** — a meta-layer that aggregates multiple registries.

### How the Kinexys Carbon System Works

The Kinexys approach to carbon credits is **native tokenisation** (as opposed to third-party bridging). This means:

```
Carbon project → Verification → Registry issuance → Native token minted directly on KDA blockchain
                                                        ↓
                                               Standardised token standard:
                                               - Vintage year
                                               - Serial number
                                               - Country
                                               - Verification body
                                               - Methodology
                                               - Project type / ID
                                               - Certification standard
```

Key features from the [JP Morgan research paper "Carbon Markets Reimagined" (June 2025)](https://www.jpmorgan.com/kinexys/documents/carbon-markets-reimagined-digital-assets.pdf):

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Unified ledger** | Single, immutable source of truth replacing fragmented registry systems |
| **DvP settlement** | Delivery-versus-payment on-chain (asset and cash settle simultaneously) |
| **Fractionalization** | Tokenised credits can be divided while maintaining full attribute traceability |
| **Price transparency** | On-chain settlement exposes pricing data for market transparency |
| **Bank-grade security** | JPMorganChase security controls throughout credit lifecycle |
| **Interoperability** | Network effects as more registries join the KDA platform |

### Seven Carbon Market Challenges KDA Addresses

JP Morgan surveyed ~40 experts across 15 companies and identified these challenges (ranked by frequency):

| Rank | Challenge | % Citing |
|------|-----------|----------|
| 1 | Lack of standardised evaluation frameworks | 24.35% |
| 2 | Reputational risk | 23.1% |
| 3 | Poor data transparency | 20.5% |
| 4 | Fragmentation | 12.82% |
| 5 | Lack of high-quality supply | 7.69% |
| 6 | Limited financial maturity | 7.69% |
| 7 | Excess low-quality supply | 3.85% |

KDA directly addresses challenges 1, 3, and 4 through standardisation, transparency, and interoperability.

### Relevance to C2050's Role

JP Morgan's paper explicitly states that **KDA is "not an authority on quality"** — it enforces standardised data structures and transparency, but does not evaluate whether a credit is genuinely additional or high-impact. This is precisely the gap C2050 claims to fill: quality evaluation, integrity assessment, legal compliance checking, and D-MRV. If the C2050/JP Morgan relationship is genuine, C2050 would likely function as a **quality layer sitting above the KDA tokenisation infrastructure**, feeding verified data into the tokens that KDA issues.

| Assessment | Coverage |
|------------|---------|
| **Platts CNC (Natural Carbon Capture)** | Nature-based removal credits (afforestation, reforestation, soil, wetlands) |
| **Platts CEC (Carbon Emission Certificates)** | Household device credits (e.g., cookstoves) |
| **Nature-Based Avoidance** | REDD+ and avoided deforestation credits |

[S&P Global Commodity Insights assessed](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/latest-news/electric-power/100925-natural-carbon-capture-removal-credit-prices-hit-2025-low-on-supply-glut) Natural Carbon Capture at **$13.30/mtCO₂e** in October 2025, with daily updates published transparently. These are described as "global benchmark price assessments backed by a transparent methodology" — analogous to how Platts prices oil or LNG.

VCM annual market value is estimated by [S&P Global Energy Horizons](https://www.spglobal.com/energy/en/news-research/blog/energy-transition/120325-how-does-the-voluntary-carbon-market-work) at **up to $4 billion by 2030**, with significant growth expected through the 2030s.

### S&P Environmental Registry and Meta Registry

A critical development: SPGCI operates its own **Environmental Registry** — a customisable registry-as-a-service platform for full-lifecycle tracking and management of carbon credits. Significantly, S&P also operates the **Meta Registry**, which integrates data from multiple existing registries (potentially including Verra, Gold Standard, CDM, and others) to create a unified data layer — directly addressing the fragmentation problem.

This makes S&P a **natural partner** for both the Kinexys tokenisation infrastructure (as confirmed by the July 2025 announcement) and for a D-MRV platform like C2050 seeking validated, multi-registry price and quality data.

### S&P Trucost

**S&P Trucost** (acquired 2016) provides:
- Environmental impact data for **20,000+ companies** across GHG emissions, water, land, pollution, and waste
- **Carbon earnings at risk** — value-at-risk indicators under different carbon pricing scenarios
- **Paris Alignment** scores — measuring how well a company's trajectory aligns with 1.5°C and 2°C scenarios
- **Physical risk** assessments based on 2.9 million+ mapped assets

Trucost's data is the engine behind S&P's ability to **price carbon risk across entire securities portfolios** — enabling institutional investors to model carbon exposure at the portfolio level. This is the "global monetisation" role in the C2050 framing: if C2050 credits eventually carry Trucost-compatible metadata, they become priceable within institutional investment portfolios.

### S&P's Role in Carbon Market Monetisation

S&P occupies a unique position as both a **price discovery mechanism** (Platts assessments) and a **financial ratings infrastructure** (S&P Ratings). Carbon instruments that receive an S&P benchmark price and carry S&P credit quality data can be integrated into:
- Structured financial products (carbon-backed securities)
- ESG indices and ETF products
- Green bond eligibility criteria
- CORSIA-eligible credit pools

## Section 5: UN Carbon Harmonisation

### Article 6 Architecture

Article 6 of the Paris Agreement provides the foundational framework for international carbon market cooperation. It has three pillars:

```
Article 6 of the Paris Agreement
│
├── Article 6.2 — Bilateral cooperative approaches
│   ├── Countries trade Internationally Transferred Mitigation Outcomes (ITMOs)
│   ├── Bilateral agreements: 97 agreements between 59 countries (as of March 2025)
│   ├── 155 pilot projects recorded
│   └── Uses existing VCM registries (Verra, Gold Standard) via the Nov 2025 Article 6.2 Crediting Protocol
│
├── Article 6.4 — UN-supervised Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism (PACM)
│   ├── Replaces Kyoto Protocol's Clean Development Mechanism (CDM)
│   ├── Overseen by the Article 6.4 Supervisory Body
│   ├── First methodology approved: October 2025 (landfill methane)
│   ├── First credits issued: February 2026 (Myanmar clean cookstoves project)
│   ├── 1,500+ new activities submitted for prior notification
│   ├── 113 Designated National Authorities (DNAs) assigned
│   └── Credits ~40% smaller than CDM equivalents due to stricter baselines
│
└── Article 6.8 — Non-market approaches
    └── Capacity building, technology transfer, policy support (no credit transfers)
```

### The February 2026 Milestone

The [UNFCCC announcement of 26 February 2026](https://unfccc.int/news/un-carbon-market-approves-first-ever-issuance-of-credits-under-the-paris-agreement) confirmed the **first-ever issuance of credits under the Paris Agreement Crediting Mechanism**:

- **Project**: Myanmar clean-cooking (efficient cookstove distribution)
- **Authorized participant**: Republic of Korea
- **Credit usage**: Korean credits flow into Korean Emissions Trading System (ETS); remainder counts toward Myanmar's NDC
- **Credits issued**: ~40% fewer than comparable CDM credits, reflecting conservative baselines
- **Significance**: Market transitions from rulemaking to live operation

As Article 6.4 Supervisory Body Chair Mkhuthazi Steleki stated: *"This initial issuance reflects the careful application of the rules set by countries under the Paris Agreement... each credited tonne genuinely represents a tonne reduced."*

A pipeline of **165+ projects** transitioning from CDM to PACM is in progress, spanning waste management, energy, industry, and agriculture across multiple regions.

### The Enhanced Transparency Framework (ETF)

The ETF, established under Article 13 of the Paris Agreement, is the reporting backbone of the global climate system. It requires all countries to submit **Biennial Transparency Reports (BTRs)** covering:
- National GHG inventories (mandatory IPCC 2006 guidelines)
- NDC tracking progress
- Climate policies and measures
- Support provided/received

The first BTRs were due by December 2024. The ETF creates a standardised, internationally comparable data layer for emission accounting — precisely the national-level framework that individual project D-MRV systems (like C2050's) must be compatible with.

### How Existing Standards Fit the Harmonisation Framework

A landmark development occurred in **November 2025**: Singapore, Verra, and Gold Standard jointly published the **[Article 6.2 Crediting Protocol](https://verra.org/singapore-gold-standard-and-verra-publish-article-6-2-crediting-protocol/)**, establishing:

- Standardised procedures for governments to use VCM registries (Verra, Gold Standard) for Article 6.2
- Standardised labelling of Article 6 status within registry systems
- Reporting guidelines for Independent Standards to support government compliance
- Roles and responsibilities for governments, standards bodies, and project developers

This is the "include rather than replace" principle in practice: VCM infrastructure is being formally integrated into the UN compliance framework, not displaced by it.

The **Integrity Council for the Voluntary Carbon Market (ICVCM)** plays a complementary governance role. Its **Core Carbon Principles (CCPs)** — 10 science-based standards for high-quality credits — are designed to interoperate with Article 6 while governing the voluntary space that Article 6 does not directly regulate. [As ICVCM states](https://icvcm.org/article-6-of-the-paris-agreement-and-the-integrity-councils-work/): *"Both CCPs and Article 6 are needed to ensure that governments and corporates can deliver high-integrity climate finance."*

## Section 6: How MGS Fits — The Green Square in a Harmonised World

### The Green Square Proposition Restated

MGS's Green Square represents **100g CO₂e** (0.0001 tCO₂e), valued at approximately **£0.003 per unit**. One standard carbon tonne (1 tCO₂e) would therefore represent **10,000 Green Squares** at a nominal value of ~£30 — broadly consistent with current voluntary credit prices for mid-quality nature-based credits (~$6–13/tCO₂e spot, or ~$26+ for premium-rated removal credits).

### Blockchain Fractionalization: Technical Feasibility

The blockchain fractionalization of carbon credits is **technically proven and commercially operational** in 2026. The architecture works as follows:

```
1. Source credit verified → Locked at registry (Verra, Gold Standard, or Article 6.4 PACM registry)
2. Token minted on blockchain (e.g., Kinexys KDA, Ethereum, Polygon)
3. Smart contract divides token into N fractional units
4. Each fraction retains full cryptographic linkage to:
   - Original project ID and serial number
   - Vintage year
   - Country of origin
   - Verification body
   - Methodology
   - Project type / co-benefits
5. Fractional units traded, transferred, or retired on-chain
6. Retirement immutably recorded → Permanent proof of offset
```

Key reference: [JP Morgan research paper (June 2025)](https://www.jpmorgan.com/kinexys/documents/carbon-markets-reimagined-digital-assets.pdf) confirms: *"Tokenized credits can be fractionalized while maintaining granular asset attributes and traceability back to their issuing projects."*

Commercial platforms are already dividing credits into **0.001 tCO₂e units** (1kg) or even smaller fractions. A 100g unit (0.0001 tCO₂e) is therefore technically feasible on modern blockchain infrastructure.

### The Traceability Stack for a £0.003 Green Square

Here is how a single Green Square could be traced end-to-end within a harmonised framework:

```
[Nature-Based Project — e.g., UK woodland]
    ↓ D-MRV (C2050's platform)
    ↓ Continuous satellite + IoT monitoring of actual sequestration
    ↓
[Verification layer]
    ↓ Quality scoring against ICVCM Core Carbon Principles
    ↓ Legal compliance check vs. Article 6 requirements
    ↓
[Registry issuance]
    ↓ Credit issued at standard 1 tCO₂e unit
    ↓ Token minted on Kinexys KDA (or compatible blockchain)
    ↓
[Fractionalization]
    ↓ Smart contract divides into 10,000 sub-tokens (each = 100g CO₂e)
    ↓ Each token = 1 Green Square
    ↓ Token metadata: project ID, vintage, methodology, location, co-benefits
    ↓
[Green Square issued to MGS customer]
    ↓ Held as digital asset / tradeable or retirable
    ↓
[Retirement / Offset claim]
    ↓ Retirement recorded immutably on blockchain
    ↓ Retirement certificate automatically generated
    ↓ Corresponding adjustment logged (if Article 6 authorised)
```

### Integration Pathway with C2050's Framework

C2050's "include rather than replace" principle is architecturally sound for MGS. The integration model would work as follows:

1. **C2050 as D-MRV layer**: C2050's SaaS platform monitors, reports, and verifies the underlying carbon-sequestering activities that generate the credits behind Green Squares. This provides the "quantifiable, verifiable, and completely traceable" quality that MGS advertises.

2. **KPMG as assurance provider**: KPMG provides third-party assurance of C2050's D-MRV outputs, giving institutional credibility to the quality claims. KPMG's advisory network introduces the framework to corporate buyers.

3. **JP Morgan's Kinexys as settlement infrastructure**: Kinexys provides the blockchain tokenisation layer, enabling fractional Green Squares to be issued, transferred, and retired with bank-grade security and price transparency.

4. **S&P Global as pricing and data layer**: S&P's Environmental Registry and Platts price assessments provide the global benchmark against which Green Squares can be valued, and Trucost data allows institutional investors to incorporate Green Square holdings into portfolio carbon accounting.

5. **Article 6 PACM as compliance anchor**: If underlying projects are registered under Article 6.4, Green Squares carry the highest level of UN-mandated environmental integrity, with corresponding adjustments preventing double-counting.

### Precedents for Micro-Unit Carbon Systems

The theoretical and commercial basis for sub-tonne carbon units is well established:

- **Thailand's TrueMoney Wallet** (Carbonmark integration, 2025): 20 million users can offset **70–930 kg of CO₂** directly in a consumer fintech app — demonstrating that sub-tonne retail carbon is operationally viable at scale ([Carbonmark, October 2025](https://www.carbonmark.com/post/building-with-blockchain-in-the-carbon-market-part-1))
- **Klima Protocol**: Manages approximately 20 million carbon credits on-chain with fractional trading capability
- **Toucan Protocol**: Enables bridging of credits with fractionalization and DeFi composability
- **Taylor & Francis (2026) academic research**: Confirms that in blockchain carbon architectures, "any value, no matter how small, can be traded... because of the traceability feature of blockchain, their contribution can be verified and traced for any future transactions" ([January 2026](https://www.tandfonline.com/doi/full/10.1080/19498241.2025.2611313))

### What Harmonisation Means for MGS

The harmonisation trend creates an increasingly favourable environment for MGS's Green Square framework:

1. **Standardisation reduces fragmentation risk**: As Article 6.4 and the Article 6.2 Protocol create common standards, Green Squares backed by these mechanisms will carry internationally recognised credentials rather than proprietary claims

2. **Blockchain infrastructure is becoming institutional-grade**: JP Morgan's entry with bank-grade security changes the risk perception of tokenised carbon for institutional buyers

3. **Quality tiers are crystallising**: The ICVCM CCP label and Article 6.4 credentials create a clear quality hierarchy — Green Squares that meet these standards will command premium pricing

4. **The "include" principle protects existing investments**: The explicit policy direction is to integrate VCM infrastructure into Article 6, not to replace it — meaning early-mover positions in high-quality VCM projects remain valuable

## Conclusions

**The macro thesis is sound.** The UN carbon market is transitioning from design to operation (first credits issued February 2026), blockchain infrastructure from JP Morgan is being tested specifically for carbon credit tokenisation with S&P Global, KPMG is actively building its carbon advisory and assurance practice, and the "include rather than replace" principle is enshrined in the Article 6.2 Crediting Protocol. **The Green Square framework is technically integrable.** Sub-tonne fractionalization to 100g CO₂e is feasible on current blockchain infrastructure. Consumer-facing retail carbon products (TrueMoney's 70–930kg CO₂ offset functionality) demonstrate the model is operationally viable at scale.

**The critical uncertainty is C2050's institutional relationships.** Until the JP Morgan, KPMG, and S&P partnerships are independently verified, MGS's investment should be treated as an early-stage bet on the right macro thesis with unconfirmed institutional backing. The company's credentials should be formally documented before any material capital deployment.
